<slang_evaluation_framework>
When evaluating slang for "BS/tricks/nonsense", ask:

1. IDIOM vs SLANG PREFERENCE
   - Is there an established idiomatic expression? (idioms often mean "straight talk" or "no beating around bush")
   - Prefer established idioms over trendy slang
   - Idioms: timeless, widely understood
   - Youth slang: may feel dated or too casual

2. AUTHENTICITY CHECK
   - Do 25-42 year olds actually use this word?
   - Does it appear in casual conversation, not just internet slang?
   - Would someone's older brother/sister (35+) understand it?
   - Critical: Is it TOO youth-specific? (under-30s only = too narrow)

3. MEANING PRECISION
   - Does it specifically mean "tricks/scams/BS/nonsense/fake promises"?
   - Or is it too generic (like "bad" or "problems" or just "lies")?
   - Best: words for deception/tricks/empty promises/fake stuff
   - Avoid: generic negative words

4. TONE CALIBRATION
   - Is it edgy enough to feel rebellious?
   - But not so vulgar it alienates mainstream casino players?
   - Sweet spot: slightly provocative but acceptable in marketing

5. MARKET COVERAGE
   - Does it work across all regions where ${targetLanguage} is spoken?
   - Or is it locked to one country/city?
   - Prefer: nationally understood across target markets

SELECTION PRIORITY:
1st choice: Established idiom with perfect meaning
2nd choice: Widely-known slang that's specific to tricks/BS
3rd choice: Clear direct translation ("without tricks")

If slang passes all 5 checks → USE IT
If slang fails check 1 or 2 → probably wrong choice
If slang fails check 3, 4, or 5 → fall back to clear direct language
</slang_evaluation_framework>
